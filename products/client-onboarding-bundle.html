Introduction

Why Client Onboarding Changes Everything

The first two weeks of a client relationship set the tone for everything that follows. Clients who feel informed, organized, and confident in your process give better feedback, respect your scope, and refer you to others. Clients who feel uncertain compensate with control — endless check-ins, scope creep, and anxiety-driven requests.

This bundle gives you the exact documents and processes that experienced freelancers use to create the first experience. Once you set up the system, onboarding a new client takes under 30 minutes — and the results speak for themselves.

How to use this bundle

Work through the four templates in order: Proposal → Contract → Welcome Packet → Kickoff Document. Each one references the previous, so a client moves through a coherent, professional sequence from first contact to active project.

The Three-Tier Pricing Method

One of the most impactful changes in this proposal template is the move from a single quote to three tiers. When clients get one price, the decision is "yes or no." When they get three options, the decision is "which one" — and 60–70% pick the middle tier.

Essential ($X)
Core deliverable with standard timeline. Limited revisions (2 rounds).
Best for: Budget-conscious clients or smaller initial engagements.

Recommended ($X × 1.4)
Everything in Essential + [key additions: extra revision round, faster timeline,
one additional asset, extended support period].
Best for: Most clients. This is the package I'd recommend for your situation.

Premium ($X × 2)
Comprehensive version — [full scope description]. Includes [VIP additions].
Best for: Clients for whom getting it perfect matters more than budget.
Pricing tip

The Essential tier isn't your throwaway option — it's what makes the Recommended tier feel like the reasonable choice. Price Essential at your actual minimum, Recommended at 1.3–1.5×, and Premium at 1.8–2.2×. Clients will rarely choose Essential or Premium — they're anchors for the middle.

The Freelance Contract

A contract that protects you without scaring clients. Written in plain English, professional in tone, and specific enough to hold up when it matters. Every clause included has a reason — no legal padding, just the clauses that prevent real problems.

Clauses included
Scope of work (with explicit exclusions)
Revision rounds and out-of-scope billing rate
Payment terms (deposit, balance, late fees)
Kill fee schedule (for client cancellation)
Intellectual property transfer (upon final payment)
Client delay clause (timeline extension trigger)
Approval and deemed-approval clause
Limitation of liability

The Kill Fee Clause (The Most Valuable Page)

If a client cancels a project you've started, you deserve compensation. Without a kill fee clause, your only protection is the deposit — and if the project was 60% complete, that may not cover your time. The kill fee creates a sliding scale based on project completion.

Cancellation and Kill Fee

If Client cancels the project after signing this agreement:

• Before work begins: Deposit is forfeited. No additional fees.
• After work begins (up to 50% complete): Client owes the deposit
  plus 25% of the remaining balance.
• After 50% completion: Client owes the deposit plus 50% of the
  remaining balance.
• After 75% completion: Client owes the deposit plus 75% of the
  remaining balance.

"Work completion" is measured by [Freelancer]'s professional assessment
of hours invested relative to total project scope.

The Client Welcome Packet

A one-page document sent when the contract is signed and deposit clears. It eliminates the most common early-project confusions: how to communicate, what happens next, what you need from them, and what they can expect. Most clients have never worked with a freelancer who sends one — which is exactly why it creates such a strong impression.

Sections included
Welcome message (personal, brief)
Communication guide (channels, response time, best way to reach you)
Project overview (stage by stage, simple language)
What I need from you (assets, access, decisions — with deadline)
What you can expect from me (timeline, check-ins, how I'll deliver)
How to give great feedback (a brief guide to the kind of input that helps)

The Feedback Guide (The Hidden Gem)

Most clients have never been taught how to give useful creative feedback. They say "make it pop" or "I'll know it when I see it." Including a brief section on how to give great feedback doesn't just help you — it signals that you run a rigorous process and take the work seriously.

How to Give Feedback That Gets Results

The most useful feedback tells me:
→ What's working (what you want to keep)
→ What's not working (and why — "this doesn't feel right" is less useful
   than "this doesn't match our brand voice, which is casual and direct")
→ Specific, not vague ("I'd like the headline to be shorter" vs. "make it
   better")
→ One round at a time — please consolidate all feedback before sending
   so we can address everything together

What to avoid:
→ Feedback that conflicts with the approved brief
→ "What would it look like if we tried [completely different direction]?"
   in round 3 of 3 (that's a scope change and we can discuss it separately)

The Kickoff Call Document

A structured intake form and kickoff agenda, combined into one document. Sent before the kickoff call so the client arrives prepared. Used during the call to guide the conversation. Converted into a project brief summary that gets emailed after. This document does the work of three separate things — and takes 20 minutes to complete per client.

Sections included
Pre-call intake questions (7 questions, covers background, goals, constraints)
Kickoff call agenda (30 or 60-minute version)
Decision log (key decisions made during the call)
Open items tracker (outstanding approvals, assets needed)
Post-call summary template (email format, sent within 24hrs)

Pre-Call Intake Questions

Send these before the kickoff call. The answers become the project brief. They also give you a reference point if the client later asks for something outside the original scope.

  1. What does success look like for this project? How will you know it worked?
  2. Who else is involved in approvals or decisions on your side?
  3. What's the single most important thing to get right? (If you could only achieve one thing, what would it be?)
  4. Are there any hard constraints I should know about — timeline, technical requirements, things that must or must not appear?
  5. What have you tried before in this area? What worked, what didn't?
  6. Are there any competitors or peers whose work in this area you admire? (For reference, not copying.)
  7. Is there anything about your business, audience, or situation that I need to understand that isn't obvious from the brief?
Complete Workflow

From Prospect to Active Project

Here's how the four templates sequence together in a complete onboarding workflow.

  • 1

    Proposal sent (Template 01)

    Client receives the three-tier proposal. Clear scope, clear pricing, one next action. You follow up once at 48 hours if no response.

  • 2

    Contract signed + deposit invoice sent (Template 02)

    Sent the moment the client selects their tier. Use a digital signature tool. Deposit invoice goes with the contract — same email. No work begins until both are complete.

  • 3

    Welcome Packet sent (Template 03)

    Sent when the deposit clears. Usually within 24 hours. Sets up communication norms, answers their most common questions, and requests any assets you need.

  • 4

    Intake form sent + kickoff call scheduled (Template 04)

    Sent with the welcome packet, or the day after. The intake questions are answered before the kickoff call. The call uses the kickoff agenda. A summary goes out within 24 hours.

  • 5

    Work begins

    You have a signed contract, a cleared deposit, an agreed brief, and a shared understanding of the project. The first deliverable can now proceed with real confidence on both sides.

Setup Guide

Customizing for Your Practice

1. Add Your Branding

Replace all instances of [Your Name] and [Your Business] with your actual name. Add your logo to the proposal and welcome packet headers. Update contact information, preferred communication channels, and payment details throughout.

2. Adjust Revision Limits

The default templates are set to 2–3 revision rounds. Adjust this based on your service type. Design work typically warrants 2–3. Writing typically warrants 2. Consulting deliverables often 1–2. The key is consistency — whatever you choose, state it clearly in both the proposal and contract.

3. Set Your Pricing Tiers

The proposal template has a three-tier pricing table with placeholder values. Fill in your actual rates for the Essential, Recommended, and Premium tiers. A rough starting point: if your standard rate is $X, Essential = $X, Recommended = $X × 1.3–1.5, Premium = $X × 1.8–2.2.

4. Adjust the Kill Fee Schedule

The kill fee percentages in the contract are suggestions. Adjust them based on your risk tolerance and typical project structure. Higher-deposit projects can use lower kill fee percentages; lower-deposit projects should use higher ones to compensate.

5. Duplicate for Every Client

In Notion, duplicate the template folder for each new client. In Google Docs, make a copy of each document per client. Never edit the master templates — keep them as your starting point.
