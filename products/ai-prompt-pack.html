Category 1: Sales & Proposals

40 prompts — write proposals, handle objections, follow up, price confidently

Writing Proposals

Prompt 1 — Proposal Opening
Write a proposal opening paragraph for a [TYPE OF PROJECT] project for [CLIENT TYPE]. The client's main goal is [GOAL]. My unique angle is [YOUR DIFFERENTIATOR]. Keep it under 80 words, confident, and client-focused (not about my background).
💡 Best on: Claude (more nuanced tone control). Works on all models.
Prompt 2 — Scope Section
Write a clear scope-of-work section for a freelance project. The deliverables are: [LIST DELIVERABLES]. The timeline is [TIMELINE]. Include what is explicitly OUT of scope to prevent scope creep. Use bullet points. Professional but plain language.
💡 The "out of scope" section is the most important part. Don't skip it.
Prompt 3 — Proposal Pricing Page
Write a pricing section for a freelance proposal. I'm offering three tiers: Tier 1 at $[PRICE] includes [INCLUSIONS], Tier 2 at $[PRICE] includes [INCLUSIONS], Tier 3 at $[PRICE] includes [INCLUSIONS]. Frame each tier by the value/outcome it delivers, not just the features. The middle tier should feel like the obvious choice.
💡 Always present three tiers. The middle option converts 60-70% of the time.
Prompt 4 — Social Proof Section
Write a social proof section for my proposal. I have these testimonials: [PASTE TESTIMONIALS]. Condense them into 2-3 impactful quotes and frame them around the client's specific concern, which is [CLIENT CONCERN]. Keep it under 150 words total.
Prompt 5 — Case Study Paragraph
Write a one-paragraph case study about a project I completed. Situation: [CLIENT TYPE] needed [PROBLEM SOLVED]. My approach: [WHAT I DID]. Result: [OUTCOME WITH NUMBERS IF POSSIBLE]. Format it as a compelling before/after story. Under 100 words.

Handling Objections

Prompt 6 — "Your rate is too high"
A client said my rate of $[RATE] is too high for a [PROJECT TYPE] project. Help me write a calm, confident response that: (1) acknowledges their concern without apologizing, (2) reframes the rate in terms of ROI or value delivered, (3) offers an alternative path if budget is genuinely constrained. Keep it under 120 words.
💡 Never lower your rate in the first response. Always reframe value first.
Prompt 7 — "We're going with someone else"
A prospect just told me they're going with another freelancer. Write a short, gracious response that: (1) congratulates them, (2) keeps the door open for future work, (3) asks one non-pushy question to understand why they chose differently (for my own learning). Under 80 words. Warm but not desperate.
Prompt 8 — "Can you do it cheaper?"
A client is asking me to reduce my price for [PROJECT TYPE]. I don't want to lower my rate. Write a response that offers to reduce the scope instead of the rate — presenting a "Good/Better/Best" option where the cheapest option has clearly defined limitations. Professional, not defensive.
Prompt 9 — "We need to think about it"
A prospect said they need to "think about it" after my proposal. Write a follow-up email to send 5 days later. It should: add value (share a relevant insight or resource), gently re-open the conversation, and make it easy to reply with a simple yes/no/still thinking. Under 100 words.
Prompt 10 — Scope Creep Response
A client is asking for [ADDITIONAL WORK] that is outside the original scope of our project. Write a professional email response that: (1) acknowledges the request positively, (2) clearly notes it's outside scope, (3) provides a quote for the additional work, and (4) keeps the relationship warm. Under 150 words.
💡 This prompt alone has saved freelancers thousands of dollars in unbilled work.

Following Up

Prompt 11 — First Follow-Up (3 days)
Write a follow-up email to a prospect 3 days after sending a proposal. I haven't heard back. The proposal was for [PROJECT TYPE]. Keep it under 60 words. Friendly, not pushy. End with a simple yes/no question to make replying easy.
Prompt 12 — "Breakup" Follow-Up (14 days)
Write a final "breakup" follow-up email to a prospect who has gone silent after my proposal. It's been 14 days with no response. Keep it under 50 words. Make it low-pressure — tell them I'm closing the file but leave the door open for the future. This often gets a response when nothing else has.
Prompt 13 — Re-engaging a Cold Lead
A prospect I spoke to [TIME PERIOD] ago went cold after seeming interested. Write an email to re-engage them. Mention a relevant industry change, development, or insight that gives me a natural reason to reach back out. Don't mention that they ghosted me. Under 100 words.

Pricing Confidence

Prompt 14 — Calculate Project Price
Help me price a freelance project. Here are the details: [PROJECT DESCRIPTION]. My hourly rate is $[RATE]. Estimated hours: [HOURS]. Account for: client meetings (usually 2-3), revisions (2 rounds), communication overhead (10%), and a 15% buffer for unknowns. Give me a recommended project price, a lower bound, and an upper bound. Explain the logic.
Prompt 15 — Rate Increase Email to Existing Client
Write an email to an existing client informing them that my rates are increasing from $[OLD RATE] to $[NEW RATE] starting [DATE]. Keep it professional, short (under 100 words), and don't apologize. Frame it as a routine business update, not a negotiation. End with confirmation that I value working with them.
Prompt 16 — Cold Outreach Email
Write a cold outreach email to [TARGET COMPANY TYPE]. I'm a freelance [ROLE] who specializes in [NICHE]. I noticed they [SPECIFIC OBSERVATION ABOUT THEIR BUSINESS]. My email should: reference that specific observation, mention one relevant result I've achieved, and make a clear low-ask CTA (15-min call, not a full proposal request). Under 100 words.
Prompt 17 — Referral Request Email
Write an email asking a satisfied client for a referral. The project I completed was [PROJECT TYPE]. The client expressed satisfaction when [POSITIVE MOMENT]. Make the ask specific — describe exactly who I'm looking for as a client, so they know who to think of. Friendly, brief (under 80 words), and not transactional.
Prompt 18 — Testimonial Request
Write a short email requesting a testimonial from a client after a successful project. Ask them to address: what problem they had, what it was like working with me, and the result they got. Make it easy by providing that structure directly in the email. Under 80 words. Warm and specific, not generic.
Prompt 19 — Discovery Call Prep
I have a discovery call with a prospect who is [COMPANY/CLIENT DESCRIPTION]. Generate 8 discovery questions I should ask to: (1) understand their real problem behind the surface request, (2) uncover budget, timeline, and decision-making process, and (3) identify red flags. Organize by category. Include 1-2 follow-up questions per main question.
Prompt 20 — Retainer Proposal
Write a proposal section for converting a project client to a monthly retainer. The project was [PROJECT TYPE]. I want to offer a retainer at $[MONTHLY PRICE] for [MONTHLY DELIVERABLES]. Frame the benefits of the retainer from the client's perspective: predictable access, priority scheduling, lower effective hourly rate. Under 200 words.

Category 2: Client Communication

35 prompts — status updates, difficult conversations, boundaries, check-ins
Prompt 21 — Weekly Status Update
Write a weekly project status update email to a client. Progress this week: [WHAT WAS COMPLETED]. Next steps: [WHAT'S COMING]. Blockers or needs from client: [ANY NEEDS]. Keep it under 120 words. Structured with clear headers. Confident and reassuring in tone.
Prompt 22 — Delay Notification
Write an email to a client informing them of a delay in the project. The original deadline was [DATE]. The new timeline is [NEW DATE]. The reason for the delay is [REASON — be honest but professional]. Include: what I'm doing to mitigate the impact and a clear new delivery date. No excessive apologizing. Under 150 words.
💡 Send this email before the deadline passes, not after. Proactive communication preserves trust.
Prompt 23 — Setting Response Time Expectations
Write a short paragraph for my client welcome email that sets expectations around response times. I typically respond within [HOURS] on [DAYS]. I'm unavailable [EXCEPTIONS]. Frame this as a professional working norm, not an apology. Under 80 words.
Prompt 24 — Handling an Unhappy Client
A client is unhappy with a deliverable. Their feedback is: [PASTE FEEDBACK]. Write a response that: (1) acknowledges their feelings without admitting fault, (2) asks clarifying questions to understand the real issue, and (3) proposes a clear next step. Do not be defensive. Under 150 words.
Prompt 25 — Invoice Follow-Up (Overdue)
Write a polite but firm follow-up email for an overdue invoice. Invoice amount: $[AMOUNT]. Due date was: [DATE]. It is now [DAYS] days overdue. This is my [FIRST/SECOND/FINAL] reminder. Each version should escalate slightly in firmness while remaining professional. Provide all three versions.
💡 Always include the invoice number and a payment link in the email body, not just an attachment.
Prompt 26 — Ending a Client Relationship
Write an email ending a professional relationship with a difficult client. I want to: (1) give [NOTICE PERIOD] notice, (2) fulfill my remaining commitments, (3) offer to help with the transition. Keep it brief, professional, and final — no room for negotiation. Under 120 words.
Prompt 27 — Requesting Feedback After Project
Write an email asking a client for feedback at the end of a project. I want to know: what went well, what I could improve, and whether they'd work with me again. Keep it conversational (not a survey). Under 80 words. Send at the moment of project completion, not later.
Prompt 28 — Rescheduling a Meeting
Write a brief email rescheduling a meeting with a client. The original time was [ORIGINAL DATE/TIME]. The reason I need to reschedule: [BRIEF REASON]. Offer 2-3 alternative times. Apologize once, briefly, and move on. Under 60 words.
Prompt 29 — Asking for a Decision
A client has been sitting on a proposal or decision for [TIME PERIOD]. Write a prompt that gently asks them to make a decision or give a timeline. Frame it around your own scheduling (not impatience): you need to plan your capacity. Under 80 words.
Prompt 30 — Declining a Project Professionally
Write a brief, professional email declining a project opportunity from [TYPE OF CLIENT/PROJECT]. Reason: [YOUR REAL REASON — budget, not the right fit, too busy]. Don't give the real reason if it's sensitive — offer a neutral explanation. Keep the relationship warm. Under 80 words. Optionally refer them to another freelancer.

Category 3: Deliverable Creation

For Copywriters

Prompt 31 — Homepage Hero Copy
Write 3 variations of homepage hero copy for [COMPANY NAME], a [BUSINESS TYPE] that helps [TARGET CUSTOMER] achieve [OUTCOME]. Each variation should have: a headline (under 10 words), a subheadline (under 25 words), and a CTA button label (under 5 words). Make each variation use a different emotional hook: #1 outcome-focused, #2 problem-focused, #3 identity-focused.
Prompt 32 — Email Subject Lines
Generate 15 email subject lines for a campaign promoting [PRODUCT/OFFER] to [AUDIENCE]. Mix the following styles: curiosity gap, direct benefit, social proof, urgency, personal/conversational. Mark which style each uses. Aim for 30-50 characters per subject line. Avoid spam trigger words.
Prompt 33 — Product Description
Write a product description for [PRODUCT NAME], a [PRODUCT TYPE] that [WHAT IT DOES]. Target customer: [WHO BUYS IT]. Main benefit: [PRIMARY BENEFIT]. Key features: [LIST 3-5 FEATURES]. Format: 2-sentence opener (benefits-led), bullet list of features (reframed as benefits), 1-sentence closer with CTA. Under 200 words total.
Prompt 34 — LinkedIn Post Draft
Write a LinkedIn post for [PERSON/BRAND] on the topic of [TOPIC]. Target audience: [AUDIENCE]. Goal: [ENGAGEMENT/THOUGHT LEADERSHIP/LEADS]. Format: hook (1 line that stops the scroll), story or insight (3-5 short paragraphs), takeaway or question to drive comments. No em dashes. Conversational, not corporate. Under 250 words.
Prompt 35 — Case Study Structure
Create an outline for a client case study. Client: [CLIENT TYPE]. Project: [PROJECT DESCRIPTION]. Result: [OUTCOME]. Structure it using the Problem → Solution → Results framework. Include: what questions to ask the client for each section, what data points to highlight, and how to handle situations where there are no quantifiable results.

For Designers

Prompt 36 — Design Brief Summary
I've gathered the following client inputs for a design project: [PASTE RAW NOTES/TRANSCRIPT]. Summarize this into a clean design brief with these sections: Project Goal, Target Audience, Key Messages, Visual Direction, Deliverables, Timeline, Success Criteria. Fill in any obvious gaps with reasonable assumptions and flag them.
Prompt 37 — Creative Concept Presentation Script
I'm presenting [NUMBER] logo/brand/design concepts to a client. Write a short verbal script for presenting each concept. For each: explain the thinking behind it, what it communicates, and what type of audience it's right for. Frame it so the client evaluates concepts strategically (not by personal taste). Under 80 words per concept.
Prompt 38 — Handling Design Feedback
A client gave me this design feedback: [PASTE FEEDBACK]. Some of it is vague or contradictory. Help me: (1) identify what they actually want vs. what they said, (2) write clarifying questions to ask before revising, and (3) draft a response acknowledging the feedback and setting up the next steps. Professional and collaborative tone.

For Developers

Prompt 39 — Technical Spec Document
Create a technical specification document outline for: [PROJECT DESCRIPTION]. Include sections for: Overview, Functional Requirements, Non-Functional Requirements (performance, security, scalability), Technical Stack Rationale, API Design, Data Models, Potential Risks, and Open Questions. Fill in what you can from the context; mark blanks as [TBD — need client input].
Prompt 40 — Project Handoff Documentation
Write a project handoff document for [PROJECT TYPE] I'm delivering to a client. Include: architecture overview, setup instructions (step by step), environment variables required, key design decisions and why I made them, known limitations, and recommended next steps. Assume the reader is a developer but not familiar with my choices. Clear and scannable.

For Consultants / Strategists

Prompt 41 — Executive Summary
Write an executive summary for a consulting report on [TOPIC] delivered to [CLIENT TYPE]. Key findings: [LIST FINDINGS]. Recommendations: [LIST RECOMMENDATIONS]. Keep it under 300 words. Start with the most important recommendation, not the methodology. Use clear, direct language — no jargon. Decision-makers should be able to act on this alone without reading the full report.
Prompt 42 — Workshop Agenda
Create a [DURATION] workshop agenda for [TOPIC] with [NUMBER OF PARTICIPANTS]. Goals: [GOALS]. Create a timed agenda with: objectives for each segment, facilitation activities, materials needed, and how to handle common workshop failures (people not participating, going off-topic, running over time). Practical and immediately usable.
Prompt 43 — Strategic Recommendation Memo
Write a one-page strategic recommendation memo for [CLIENT] on whether they should [DECISION/OPTION]. Arguments for: [LIST]. Arguments against: [LIST]. My recommendation: [YOUR RECOMMENDATION]. Frame it as a neutral analysis, then give a clear recommendation with reasoning. Use headers. Under 400 words.

Prompt 49 — Annual Income Projection

Planned capacity change: [MORE/FEWER HOURS]. Model three scenarios: conservative (no growth), moderate (10% growth), and ambitious (25% growth). Include monthly breakdowns and key assumptions.
Prompt 50 — Capacity Planning
I have [TOTAL HOURS/WEEK] available for client work. I currently have these commitments: [LIST PROJECTS WITH HOURS/WEEK]. A new opportunity needs [HOURS/WEEK] for [DURATION]. Should I take it? How to Get Better Results — Advanced Tips

Tip 1: Chain prompts for complex deliverables.

For long documents (proposals, case studies, reports), don't try to generate everything at once. Run Prompt A to create the outline, then run Prompt B to expand each section. You'll get 3x better output.

Tip 2: Use the "Role" prefix for better expertise.

Start any prompt with: "Act as a senior [ROLE] with 15 years of experience in [INDUSTRY]." This dramatically improves the quality of strategic and advisory outputs.

Tip 3: Claude vs ChatGPT for different tasks.

Claude (Anthropic) tends to be better for: nuanced tone, following complex instructions, and avoiding generic-sounding output. ChatGPT tends to be better for: brainstorming lists, code-adjacent tasks, and when you want more variations quickly. Both work with every prompt in this pack — try both when quality matters.

Tip 4: Ask for format explicitly.

Always specify: word count, number of options, and format (bullets / paragraphs / headers). Vague prompts get vague outputs. "Under 100 words, 3 variations, bullet points" produces better results than "write something short."
