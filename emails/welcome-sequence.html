Hi {{first_name | default: "there"}},

Quick question: do you actually know your minimum viable hourly rate?

Not the rate you charge — the rate you *need* to charge to hit your income goal after taxes, overhead, and realistic billable hours.

Most freelancers don't know this number. They pick a rate that "feels right" or copy what they've seen others charge. The result: they work full-time hours and earn part-time income.

Here's how to calculate it:

Annual income goal ÷ (1 − tax rate) = gross income needed
Gross income needed × (1 + overhead %) = gross revenue needed
Gross revenue needed ÷ (working weeks × billable hrs/week) = your minimum hourly rate

The uncomfortable finding for most people: their current rate is the "minimum," not the "recommended."

The "any updates?" email.

If you freelance for more than a month, you'll get this. Clients asking where the project stands, whether you've started, when they'll see something.

Every one of these emails costs you 5–10 minutes of lost focus. They happen because clients feel uncertain.

The fix takes 20 minutes to set up once:

Create a shared Notion page for each client. On it: the project name, current stage, what's in progress, and what they're waiting on. Give them the link. Update it once a week.

When a client has a live view of their project, the check-in emails stop. Not because you asked them to stop emailing — because they have what they were looking for.

Bonus effect: clients who feel informed tend to give better feedback. They're not anxious, so they're more thoughtful.

Email 4 — Day 14 (Proposal/sales lesson)

Delay: 14 days after Day 0

Subject line options:

  • The proposal mistake that kills deals silently
  • Why three prices close more than one
  • How to structure a proposal that wins
Hi {{first_name | default: "there"}},

Most freelance proposals start with "About Me."

That's a mistake.

The client already knows who you are — they found you somehow and asked for a proposal. What they don't know yet is whether you understand their problem.

The first section of a winning proposal isn't your bio. It's their problem, described accurately.

One paragraph. What are they trying to solve? What's not working? What do they stand to gain?

When a client reads their own problem described back to them with precision, they stop looking for reasons to hesitate. You've already demonstrated the thing they hired you to prove: that you understand their situation.

Three other things that move proposals from "maybe" to "yes":

1. Three price tiers instead of one price. When you give one number, the decision is "yes or no." When you give three (Essential / Recommended / Premium), the decision becomes "which one" — and most people choose the middle. This alone increased my close rate significantly.

2. Explicit scope exclusions. "What's not included" is as important as what is. It's the section that prevents the "but I thought..." conversations three weeks into a project.

3. One clear next action at the end. Not "let me know if you have questions" — "reply to this email to accept the Recommended package and I'll send the contract and deposit invoice."
