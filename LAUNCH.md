### r/freelance post (value-first, no hard sell)

**Title:** I interviewed 200+ six-figure freelancers. Here are the 5 systems they all had in common.

**Body:**
I spent the last few months interviewing freelancers earning between $60K and $400K/year, trying to understand what separated those who were profitable and calm from those who were always scrambling.

Here's what almost every high earner had:

**1. A real CRM.** Not a spreadsheet they never opened — an actual place to track prospects, follow-up dates, and notes. The freelancers who followed up consistently closed 3x more leads.

**2. A client portal.** Something to share with clients that showed project status without them needing to email you asking "where are we?" Reduces check-in emails by about 80%.

**3. A standard proposal structure.** Not starting from scratch every time. A template they could customize in 20 minutes, with a clear pricing presentation.

**4. Invoice tracking that told them their actual hourly rate.** Most freelancers don't know this number. When you link time logged → contract value → hours spent, you find out which clients are actually profitable.

**5. A weekly review ritual.** 15 minutes every Friday answering 5 questions. What shipped, what's stalled, revenue check, top priority next week. Compounded over months, this is enormous.

None of this is revolutionary. But most freelancers don't have any of it in one place, connected.

I ended up building a Notion template around these patterns — it's at freelancekit.co if you want to see the structure. But even if you build your own version, these five things will make a meaningful difference.

### r/Notion post (template-focused)

**Title:** I built a complete freelancer business OS in Notion after talking to 200 independent contractors — here's the structure

**Body:**
After months of interviewing working freelancers about how they manage their businesses, I noticed a clear pattern: the most profitable ones had connected systems. The struggling ones had disconnected ones (or none at all).

Here's the database structure I landed on for a complete freelancer Notion workspace:

**The 5 linked databases:**

1. **Client CRM** — Prospect → Active → Complete pipeline with source tracking, last contact date, and rollup total billed from invoices
2. **Project Hub** — One row per project, linked to client. Status, deadline, contract value, and a profitability formula (contract value ÷ hours = effective hourly rate)
3. **Task Manager** — All tasks linked to projects. The "Today" filtered view becomes your daily driver
4. **Invoice Tracker** — Every invoice sent. Grouped by status. Shows outstanding vs. paid at a glance
5. **Client Portal** — A shareable Notion page (no Notion account required to view) that clients use to see project status without emailing you

The key insight: these need to be **linked**, not separate databases. When you link invoices → clients, you get "Total Billed" as a rollup. When you link tasks → projects, you get hours logged automatically. The whole thing becomes more than the sum of its parts.

I packaged this up as a template at freelancekit.co ($29). But if you want to build your own version, this structure is the foundation.

Happy to answer questions about the Notion setup if people have them.
